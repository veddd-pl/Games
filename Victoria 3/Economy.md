Victoria 3 is often described as a "society simulator" where the economy is the engine. Unlike many strategy games where money is just a resource you collect, in Victoria 3, the economy is a circular flow of goods, labor, and capital.

Here is the breakdown of how the core systems work together:

### 1. The Core Economic Loop: The "Construction Snowball"
The most important thing to understand is that **Construction** drives growth.
* **The Loop:** You build **Construction Sectors** $\rightarrow$ they use goods like Wood, Iron, and Tools $\rightarrow$ you build mines and factories to make those goods cheaper $\rightarrow$ because the goods are cheaper, you can afford more Construction Sectors $\rightarrow$ you build even faster.
* **GDP:** Your Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total value of all goods produced in your country. As you build more factories and mines, your GDP rises, which increases your **Minting** (free money from having a large economy) and your tax base.

### 2. Market and Prices (Supply vs. Demand)
Prices in Victoria 3 are not fixed; they are determined by the balance of **Buy Orders** and **Sell Orders** in your National Market.
* **The Golden Rule:** You want the inputs for your factories (like Coal for Steel) to be **cheap** (excess supply) and the outputs they sell (like Steel) to be **expensive** (high demand). This makes the building profitable.
* **MAPI (Market Access Price Impact):** Goods are cheaper in the state where they are produced. To maximize efficiency, try to build "Industrial Clusters." For example, build your Steel Mills in the same state as your Iron and Coal mines to avoid losing money to "local price" inefficiencies.

### 3. The "Pops" (Your Workforce)
Every person in your country is a "Pop" with a profession (Peasant, Laborer, Machinist, Capitalist).
* **De-peasanting:** At the start, most of your people are **Peasants** living on subsistence farms. They pay almost no taxes and buy almost nothing. Your goal is to build factories to turn them into **Laborers** and **Machinists**.
* **Standard of Living (SoL):** As Pops earn higher wages, they buy more goods (Clothes, Meat, Furniture). This creates "Consumer Demand," which keeps your factories profitable.
* **Qualifications:** If you build a high-tech factory but your Pops are illiterate, they won't have the "Qualifications" to work as Engineers, and the building will sit empty.

### 4. Money and Budget
Your budget isn't just a bank account; it’s a tool for reinvestment.
* **The Investment Pool:** Depending on your laws (like *Laissez-Faire*), your wealthy Pops (Capitalists and Aristocrats) will put a portion of their profits into a private fund. This fund automatically pays for the construction of new private buildings, essentially giving you "free" growth.
* **Deficit Spending:** For major powers, "being in the red" isn't always bad. If your economy is growing faster than your interest rates, you can effectively "borrow from the future" to build your industry now.
* **Taxation:** Early game taxes usually target the poor (Land-based/Per Capita). As you modernize, you'll want to move to **Proportional** or **Graduated** taxation to capture the massive wealth of your growing middle and upper classes.

### 5. Production Methods (The "Green Buttons")
Every building has "Production Methods" (PMs) that change how it functions. 
* **Automation:** Some PMs (like adding Steam Donkeys to mines) reduce the number of workers needed but increase the use of Coal and Tools. Use these if you have a labor shortage or high wages.
* **Inputs:** Some PMs change what the building makes (e.g., a Furniture Manufactory making Luxury Furniture instead of Basic Furniture). Match these to what your wealthy Pops are actually demanding.
